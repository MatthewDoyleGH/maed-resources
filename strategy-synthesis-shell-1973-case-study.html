Scenario Synthesis Process — Royal Dutch Shell, 1973 Oil Shock

Applying the scenario-based synthesis chain to Pierre Wack and Ted Newland's Group Planning work at Shell, 1971–1974

From Uncertainty to Robust Commitment — The Case that Defined a Field

In the early 1970s, Royal Dutch Shell's Group Planning department, led by Pierre Wack and Ted Newland, began publishing a new kind of document. At the time, oil cost roughly $2 per barrel, the Seven Sisters (the seven major Western oil companies) controlled around 85% of reserves outside the USSR and China, and the consensus view across Shell's senior management — and across virtually every competitor — was that cheap oil would continue to flow indefinitely. Wack and Newland's documents invited managers to imagine a different future: one in which the producer-state governments in the Middle East would act collectively, use oil as a political instrument, and push prices multiple times higher. It was not presented as a forecast. It was presented as a scenario — a coherent, internally consistent, plausible alternative history of the next decade, placed alongside the consensus forecast with equal weight.

When the October 1973 Yom Kippur War triggered the Arab oil embargo and OPEC quadrupled crude prices within months, Shell was the only one of the Seven Sisters whose senior managers had already mentally rehearsed that exact outcome. Over the following decade, Shell rose from seventh-largest oil major to second-largest, and Pierre Wack\'s method — what he later called "the gentle art of re-perceiving" — became the founding text of scenario planning as a corporate discipline. Every scenario-planning toolkit, every foresight programme, every strategic-uncertainty methodology taught in business schools today traces its lineage through this case.

This map applies the scenario-based synthesis chain retrospectively to Wack and Newland\'s 1971–1974 work. A Level 7 honest reading has to resist two common simplifications. First, Wack did not predict the oil shock — he made the possibility thinkable by placing it on the management table with equal weight to the consensus forecast. Second, the legend has partly obscured Wack\'s own most important methodological insight: his early scenarios failed because managers "saw them but did not believe them". The second generation of scenarios worked only because they changed how managers perceived, not just what they knew. This case walks through both the process and Wack\'s own mid-course correction — because the correction is the real lesson.

In the scenario variant of the synthesis chain, Stage 1 is a driving-forces scan. Instead of cataloguing macro trends as independent PESTEL items, the scan sorts every driver into one of two categories: predetermined elements (things we are certain will happen) and critical uncertainties (things that could go multiple ways and whose outcomes would change the strategy). Wack insisted on this distinction because, in his words, "the purpose of scenarios is not to analyse uncertainty — it is to separate the certain from the uncertain so that managers can think clearly about both."

Stage 2 in the scenario variant is an internal readiness audit. The question is not "what are our competitive advantages?" but "how would each of our major capital commitments, operating contracts, and organisational decisions perform under each of the plausible futures we are about to construct?" For Shell in 1971–72, the audit identified something crucial: much of the downstream portfolio (refineries, fixed-price customer contracts, tanker fleet commitments, forecourt concessions) had been built on assumptions that were only valid under the consensus forecast of $2 oil forever.

In the scenario variant, Stage 3 replaces SWOT with scenario construction. The driving-forces from Stage 1 are combined into two (or sometimes three) coherent, internally consistent alternative futures. Wack\'s discipline was that both scenarios had to be plausible enough to take seriously — which required refusing to label one "probable" and the other "unlikely". Presenting them side by side with equal weight was the whole point: it forced managers to prepare for Scenario B even though their instincts told them Scenario A was more likely.
Scenario A — "Business As Usual"
Consensus forecast. Oil continues at ~$2/barrel. Demand grows at 7% per annum. Supply expansion from the Middle East matches demand. No coordinated OPEC action because producer states will defect from any cartel (the pattern up to 1970). Seven Sisters continue to control the global pricing mechanism. Shell\'s downstream commitments (refineries, fixed-price customer contracts, tanker fleet) remain profitable on their original economics. Business planning is extrapolation from the last decade.
Scenario B — "Rapids"
OPEC member states act collectively for the first time. Producer governments nationalise oil reserves and pricing. The price rises sharply and discontinuously — from $2 to $5 to $10 or higher within a few years. Oil becomes a political weapon in Middle East conflicts. Demand eventually softens as consumer economies adjust. The Seven Sisters lose pricing control. Downstream commitments tied to the old $2 oil world are upside-down. Refining margins shift. Demand destruction becomes real for the first time.
Implications under Scenario A
Existing refining and distribution plans remain valid. Capital expansion continues at historic pace. No change to downstream contract structure. Minor adjustments for gradual Middle East supply increase. Competitive position stable. This is the business plan Shell would have produced in 1971 if Group Planning had not intervened.
Implications under Scenario B
Refineries must be ready to process heavier, sourer crude from non-traditional sources. Fixed-price customer contracts become a liability if crude prices rise sharply. Balance sheet must be strong enough to survive demand destruction. Country Chairmen must be pre-briefed so they can take pricing decisions without head office approval during a fast-moving crisis. Non-OPEC acreage becomes strategically critical (North Sea, Alaska, etc.). The whole downstream economic model requires restructuring.

In the scenario variant, Stage 4 replaces the TOWS Matrix with a robust commitment matrix. Instead of cross-matching Strengths/Weaknesses against Opportunities/Threats, it tests each candidate commitment against each scenario and sorts them into four categories: Robust (good under both scenarios), Contingent (good under one scenario and neutral or manageable under the other), Optional (small bets that pay off only under a specific scenario but can be cheaply reversed), and Avoid (costly under at least one scenario). The four cells map naturally onto the TOWS grid structure, which is why the synthesis chain can still use the grid — but the logic is scenario-driven, not competitor-driven.

The output of the entire synthesis chain
The output of the scenario synthesis was not a single strategic plan with quantified targets. It was a programme of rehearsals: structured conversations, briefing documents, and tabletop exercises designed to make Shell\'s senior management mentally ready to respond if Scenario B materialised. Wack understood that a scenario\'s value is measured not by whether it correctly predicts the future, but by whether it changes how managers perceive, decide, and act when reality unfolds. Shell\'s Country Chairmen arrived at October 1973 already knowing what to do.

The Synthesis Principle — Scenarios Change Perception, Not Just Knowledge
The defining feature of the Shell 1973 case is something Pierre Wack wrote about with great candour in his 1985 Harvard Business Review articles ("Scenarios: Uncharted Waters Ahead" and "Scenarios: Shooting the Rapids"). Wack\'s first-generation scenarios in 1971–72 had technically described the price-shock scenario in useful detail. Managers read the scenarios. They filed them. They went back to running the business on the consensus forecast. Wack\'s own summary was devastating: "We saw it but we didn\'t believe it." The scenarios had transmitted knowledge without changing perception.

The breakthrough came with the second generation, built in 1972–73. Wack changed how the scenarios were presented — through workshop rehearsals, face-to-face briefings with every Country Chairman, and a deliberate attempt to make managers feel what the scenario would be like on the day they had to make decisions under it. The point was not to tell managers "here is what might happen" but to let them imagine themselves inside the scenario, making the calls they would actually have to make. When October 1973 arrived, Shell\'s Country Chairmen were not working from memory — they were executing conversations they had already had.

The governance lesson: scenario planning is not forecasting. It is not risk management. It is not option valuation. It is structured re-perception — a way to let senior managers encounter alternative futures vividly enough that they can behave differently the moment the future starts to unfold. A board auditing a scenario-planning exercise should not ask "are the probabilities right?" or "are the numbers credible?" — it should ask "do our senior managers now behave differently because they have rehearsed these scenarios?" If yes, the method has worked. If no, the method has failed regardless of how elegant the scenarios are on paper.

A Level 7 honest reading also has to name what the legend obscures. First, Wack did not predict 1973. He made 1973 thinkable. Those are very different claims, and the popular version of the story tends to collapse them. Second, Shell\'s corporate-scale success was not purely the result of the scenarios — it also required a vertically integrated portfolio, a conservative balance sheet, a culture that accepted Group Planning as a legitimate voice, and a C-suite prepared to act on uncomfortable findings. Scenarios that arrive in an organisation unwilling to act on them (see the UK\'s Exercise Cygnus pandemic-preparedness scenarios before 2020 as a cautionary counterpoint) produce no strategic benefit. The method works only when the governance is ready to meet it halfway. Third, many organisations adopted scenario planning after 1973 and ran it once, filed the report, and never changed behaviour. The method\'s 50-year track record in companies that have actually sustained it (Shell itself, a handful of central banks, the Singapore government, a few long-lived foundations) is much more impressive than its track record in companies that treated it as a one-off consulting exercise. The difference is not the quality of the scenarios — it is whether the governance infrastructure around them is built to rehearse, revisit, and act.
